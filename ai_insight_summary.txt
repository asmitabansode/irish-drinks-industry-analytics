## Irish Drinks Exports: A Summary

Irish drinks exports showed strong underlying growth between 2019 and 2024, though the path was far from smooth. Total exports stood at approximately EUR 1.6 billion in 2019 before falling sharply to EUR 1.3 billion in 2020, a drop of around 19%, largely reflecting the disruption to on-trade and travel retail during the pandemic. A strong rebound followed, with exports climbing back through 2021 and reaching an approximate EUR 2.0 billion in 2022. A pullback to EUR 1.8 billion in 2023 interrupted that momentum, but 2024 saw exports recover to EUR 2.1 billion, a gain of around 17% on the prior year and the highest level in the series. Beer held broadly steady across the period, finishing at EUR 325 million in 2024, while cream liqueur grew from EUR 364 million to EUR 380 million.

Whiskey is the single largest category and has driven much of the overall story. From a 2019 base of EUR 825 million, whiskey exports fell to EUR 620 million in 2020, a decline of nearly 25%. The recovery was swift, with the category reaching approximately EUR 1.0 billion in 2022 before easing to EUR 875 million in 2023, a fall of 12.5%. By 2024, whiskey had returned to EUR 1.0 billion, matching its earlier peak. The category now accounts for roughly half of all Irish drinks exports, underscoring how heavily the overall performance depends on whiskey demand in key markets such as the United States and Europe.

The near-term outlook is cautiously positive. A directional forecast points to total exports rising to around EUR 2.17 billion in 2025 and EUR 2.29 billion in 2026, suggesting continued, if moderate, growth. However, these projections should be treated with care. The data series covers only six complete years, which is a short window that includes the highly unusual shock of 2020. That limits the reliability of any trend-based forecast, and the figures do not account for potential headwinds such as shifts in consumer preferences, currency movements, or changes in trade policy.
